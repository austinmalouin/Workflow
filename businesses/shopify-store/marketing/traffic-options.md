# RIDGEWOOD Traffic-Driving Options — Cost/Effort Comparison

**Date**: 2026-07-22
**Why this exists**: RIDGEWOOD has had 14 sessions in 30 days (12 direct, 2 organic Google —
essentially just Austin loading the site), $0 sales ever, and $0 marketing spend. The store has
zero real customer proof yet. Before spending money or time, Austin needs an honest comparison of
how to get real traffic, not a list of generic ideas. This document grounds every cost figure in
2026 market research (cited inline) rather than assumption.

---

## 0. Starting constraints that shape every option below

- **31 SKUs, 30 dropshipped via StorePilot, 0 sales ever.** No customer photos, no reviews, no
  proof of demand for *this specific catalog*. The fabricated reviews section was just removed
  (2026-07-23) — the store currently shows zero social proof, which is honest but converts worse.
- **Weapons-adjacent SKUs materially restrict paid options.** The "Adventure Ready Tactical Axe"
  and the knife SKUs are the kind of product that gets ad accounts rejected or suspended, not just
  individual ads rejected, on the two biggest paid channels:
  - **Meta**: prohibits hunting knives, tactical knives, swords, and spears outright; only
    culinary knives are allowed. ([AdAmigo.ai](https://www.adamigo.ai/blog/meta-ads-policy-faq-weapons-explosives))
  - **Google Ads**: explicitly names "throwing axes," military knives, and fighting knives as
    prohibited knife categories, and separately prohibits any product "designed to injure an
    opponent in sport, self-defense, or combat." ([Google Ads Policy Help](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/16489730?hl=en))
  - **TikTok**: bans weapon/knife ad content globally, with a narrow 18+ exception in a list of
    European countries only — irrelevant for a US-based store. ([TikTok Ads Policy](https://ads.tiktok.com/help/article/tiktok-ads-policy-dangerous-products-or-services))
  - **Practical implication**: the axe and knife SKUs cannot be advertised on any major paid
    platform, and including them in a Meta/Google catalog-wide feed risks the *whole ad account*
    getting flagged during review, not just those items. They need to be excluded from any Shopping
    feed, Meta catalog, or ad creative used for paid traffic — this is a merchandising decision to
    make before running paid, not an ads-manager setting to tweak after rejection.
- **Niche is a saturated, low-differentiation dropship category.** Outdoor/camping/tactical gear
  built on a "StorePilot"-style catalog is a commodity fight against thousands of visually
  identical stores — this raises both organic and paid difficulty versus a defensible niche.
- **Single operator, $0 marketing spend to date.** Time is the real currency here as much as
  dollars — every "free" organic channel still consumes Austin's hours.

## 1. Organic-only path ($0 hard cost, real time cost)

| Channel | What it takes | Timeline to meaningful traffic | Difficulty in this niche |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SEO / content rewrite** | Rewrite 31 product pages with real differentiated copy, add buying-guide/blog content, fix technical SEO (titles, schema, internal linking), build some backlinks | **New domains commonly take 6–12+ months to show meaningful ranked traffic**; established sites see movement in 30–90 days, but RIDGEWOOD has no domain history, no backlinks, no indexed content of substance. Realistic first-meaningful-traffic window: **4–6 months minimum for any movement, 9–12 months for anything durable**. ([SEO.com](https://www.seo.com/blog/how-long-does-seo-take/), [Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/guide/how-long-does-seo-take-to-work)) | High. Generic dropship product titles ("Tactical Multi-Tool X200") compete against Amazon, REI, and thousands of other AliExpress-style stores with the same photos and near-identical copy. Ranking requires genuine differentiation (buying guides, comparison content, a narrower angle) that a single operator can produce but slowly. |
| **TikTok organic** | Post short-form video (product demos, use-cases, "gear check" style content) consistently, ideally daily/near-daily | Creator accounts can occasionally hit viral reach in weeks, but **reliable, repeatable traffic** from organic TikTok for an unknown brand realistically takes **8–16 weeks of consistent posting** before a discernible pattern emerges, and most accounts never break out without paid boost or an existing audience. | High-variance: a single viral video *can* send thousands of sessions overnight (this is the upside case), but it's a lottery, not a plan — cannot be scheduled or guaranteed. Content must look native (UGC-style, not ad-like), which takes real filming/editing time per post, not just copy-paste of product photos. |
| **Email capture** | Add a popup/signup incentive, build a welcome sequence | Immediate to set up, but **useless without traffic** — it's a conversion/retention layer, not a traffic source. With ~14 sessions/month, an email list would grow by maybe 1–2 signups a month at a generous 10% capture rate. | Not applicable as a standalone traffic driver — this compounds value from the other two channels rather than generating new visitors itself. |

**Total organic verdict**: $0 hard cost, but real time cost (product copy rewrite: ~15–25 hours;
ongoing TikTok posting: 3–6 hours/week indefinitely; SEO content: several hours/week ongoing).
Expect **no meaningful traffic for 2–4 months even in a good-execution scenario**, and this niche's
saturation means "meaningful" may still be modest (dozens, not hundreds, of weekly sessions) without
a real differentiation angle. TikTok organic is the only piece of this with a plausible fast
upside, and it's a low-probability lottery ticket, not a base-case plan.

**Reading these numbers for RIDGEWOOD specifically**: the optimistic $25 dropshipping-CAC figure
assumes an already-optimized funnel and known-converting creative — neither exists yet here. A
more realistic first-campaign expectation for a brand-new store with zero proof is closer to the
**$60–$150+ CAC range** until creative/targeting is proven out, because early spend is mostly paying
for the learning phase (finding the audience and angle that convert), not efficient bottom-funnel
clicks.

### Platform viability given the weapons-policy issue

- **Google Shopping/Search**: viable for the ~28 non-weapon SKUs (flashlights, hammocks, backpacks,
  cutlery sets, whistles, camp lighting) if the axe and knives are excluded from the product feed.
  Cheapest CPC of the three paid channels.
- **Meta**: same exclusion required; Meta is stricter and reviews the whole catalog, so leaving
  weapon SKUs live anywhere in the connected catalog/feed risks a broader account flag, not just
  those listings.
- **TikTok paid**: same exclusion; TikTok's ecommerce CPC (~$0.79 retail) is competitive with Meta,
  and the channel is already enabled and unused, but organic-vs-paid TikTok both require native
  video content, which is a production task either way.
- **Net effect**: paid is fully usable for the outdoor-gear core of the catalog once weapon SKUs
  are carved out of any ad feed — this isn't a reason to avoid paid, but it does mean "run ads for
  the whole store" isn't a valid plan as-is.

### Realistic minimum viable test budget

Multiple sources converge on **$500–$1,000/month per platform** as a floor for a real signal, with
**30–60 days minimum** before judging a channel, since early spend goes to the algorithm's learning
phase. ([trackbee.io](https://www.trackbee.io/blog/tiktok-ads-cost-cpc-cpm-budget-best-strategies))
For a single platform test at RIDGEWOOD's likely $60–$150 CAC range, $500 buys somewhere between
3–8 actual customers — genuinely a "does this work at all" test, not a scalable acquisition engine.

**Recommended MVT budget**: **$600–$800 total, one platform at a time, over 4–6 weeks**, not split
across three channels simultaneously — splitting a small budget three ways means none of them
reaches statistical signal. Google Shopping is the logical first platform given lowest CPC and
least policy friction for this catalog.

## 3. Hybrid path — the sensible phased combination

Given a single operator and $0 current spend, a phased hybrid sequencing organic groundwork ahead
of paid spend avoids paying to send traffic into an unproven, zero-proof storefront:

**Phase 0 (weeks 1–2, $0): Fix the shop window before inviting anyone in.**
Rewrite product copy for the ~10 best/most-differentiated SKUs first (not all 31), exclude the axe
and knives from any future ad feed, decide the tea-SKU brand-fit question, set up basic email
capture. This is prerequisite hygiene, not a growth channel — but running paid traffic at the
store's current state (generic copy, no reviews, mixed niche) would waste the ad spend.

**Phase 1 (weeks 2–6, $0 + time): Start organic simultaneously.**
Begin TikTok organic posting (3–5x/week) and start SEO content (buying guides, not just product
pages) in parallel — both need lead time before they pay off, so start the clock now regardless of
the paid decision.

**Phase 2 (week 5–6 onward, ~$600–$800/month): Layer in one paid channel.**
Once product pages are rewritten and at least a few weeks of organic content exist (so paid traffic
doesn't land on a bare page), start the Google Shopping MVT on the non-weapon SKUs. Hold for a full
4–6 week test before judging or adding a second channel.

**Phase 3 (month 3+): Expand based on evidence.**
If Google Shopping produces sales at a workable CAC, scale it and/or add Meta or TikTok paid using
the same excluded-catalog rule. If TikTok organic is showing early traction, consider TikTok paid
next (boost what's already proven to resonate, rather than paying to discover creative from zero).
If nothing paid is working by month 3–4, that's a signal to revisit product/differentiation, not to
keep increasing spend.

## 4. Recommendation for RIDGEWOOD specifically

**Start organic-first, immediately, at $0 — and hold off on paid spend until Phase 0 hygiene is
done (roughly 2–4 weeks out), then run one small, single-platform paid test rather than a multi-
channel launch.**

Reasoning specific to this store's actual situation, not generic advice:

1. **There is no evidence yet that this catalog converts at all.** Zero sales across any traffic
   source to date means the honest first question isn't "which channel is cheapest" but "does
   anyone want this, presented this way." Sending paid traffic at $60–$150 CAC into an unproven
   funnel risks spending real money to learn what a week of honest self-review (Phase 0) could
   surface for free.
2. **The weapons-policy issue isn't a minor footnote — it's a merchandising decision that has to
   happen before any paid spend**, since running ads with the axe/knives in the feed risks account-
   level rejection on both Meta and Google, not just those two listings.
3. **Single operator + $0 spend today means time is the binding constraint, not money** — the
   organic channels cost nothing but hours, and starting them now (while Phase 0 hygiene work
   happens anyway) means the 2–4 month SEO/TikTok lead time is already running by the time paid
   testing starts, instead of stacked sequentially after.
4. **The realistic MVT budget ($600–$800/month, one platform) is small enough to actually afford
   out of pocket** for a pre-revenue single-operator store, and Google Shopping is the correct first
   platform: lowest CPC of the three (~$0.66), least policy friction once weapon SKUs are excluded,
   and shopping intent traffic converts better for a product-led store than social discovery traffic
   does.
5. **Do not run all three paid channels at once.** With no revenue yet, splitting a small test
   budget three ways guarantees none of them produces a clean signal — sequential testing, one
   channel at a time, is the only version of "paid" that a single-operator pre-revenue store can
   actually learn from.

**Bottom line**: this is a traffic problem with a two-track fix — free-but-slow (organic, 2–4 months
to any signal) and fast-but-costly (paid, ~$600–800/month minimum test, real CAC risk on an unproven
funnel). The right sequencing for RIDGEWOOD is organic starting now, paid starting in 3–6 weeks once
the storefront itself is worth paying to send people to.
