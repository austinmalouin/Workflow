# Houses of the Throne — 90-Day (13-Week) YouTube Growth Roadmap

**Prepared**: 2026-07-23 | **Status**: pre-launch, zero subscribers, zero videos, zero server population
**Owner**: Austin (solo creator) | **Stack**: OBS (record) + CapCut (edit) + real or AI narration, $0 software cost

This roadmap is grounded in July 2026 research on how YouTube's recommendation systems and the
Minecraft-content ecosystem actually behave right now (see Sources at bottom), not generic
templated creator advice. It assumes reality, not best-case: a brand-new channel in a low-RPM
niche, one person doing every job, and a server that has to be populated by the same content that's
supposed to promote it.

---

## The three constraints that shape everything below

1. **Server cold-start.** Zero players means the first 2-3 weeks of "content" cannot be genuine
   server-recap footage — there's no rivalry, no siege, no betrayal yet because nobody has joined.
   Early episodes have to be pre-population (lore reveal, world tour, House-founding by Austin +
   any early recruits) — real narrative material doesn't exist until players show up, which is
   itself downstream of the channel existing. This roadmap sequences around that chicken-and-egg
   problem explicitly (see Week 1-4).
2. **Solo-creator load.** One person scripting + recording + narrating + editing + thumbnailing
   is a real bottleneck. Research on creator burnout is consistent: a single long-form video
   typically costs 6-12 hours of work beyond filming, and roughly 90% of creators report burnout
   symptoms within their first year. The cadence below is deliberately conservative — 1 long-form
   video every 1-2 weeks for the first month, not weekly — because a missed "planned weekly" upload
   does more algorithmic and morale damage than a correctly-set expectation of biweekly.
3. **Competitive niche, low RPM.** Minecraft gameplay/Let's Play content runs a real RPM of
   roughly $1.50-$3.50 (gaming overall $1-$3.50), well below finance/insurance-tier niches —
   revenue only works at real volume. Kingdom/faction-war content (Empires SMP) proved the format
   draws an audience, but Empires launched with 10-12 established creators already carrying
   audiences on day one; this channel launches with none of that built-in reach. Expect a slower
   cold-start than Empires' trajectory implies.

---

## Realistic milestone math (read this before setting expectations)

- **1,000 subscribers**: niche-focused channels with strong SEO and consistent hybrid
  (long-form + Shorts) posting can hit this in **3-6 months**; average new channels take
  6-18 months; channels without a clear content identity often never get there. This channel has
  a genuine niche identity (serialized faction drama) working in its favor, but zero built-in
  audience working against it — **treat 4-7 months as the realistic window for 1,000 subs**, not
  90 days.
- **4,000 public watch hours**: channels posting 3-4 long-form videos/week hit this in 3-6 months;
  channels adding weekly live content can get there in 1-3 months; Shorts-only channels can take
  12-18 months. At this channel's realistic biweekly-to-weekly long-form cadence, **6-9 months is
  the honest estimate** for 4,000 hours, assuming episodes hold retention.
- **Combined monetization eligibility (both thresholds)**: realistically **5-9 months from
  launch**, not 90 days, assuming consistent posting and no viral break. **90 days will not hit
  monetization for this channel** — the goal of this roadmap is to get the channel to the point
  where that timeline is plausible, not to hit it by day 90. State this to Austin plainly so
  nobody is disappointed at day 90 for the right reasons.
- **First-month numbers will be small, honestly**: expect low hundreds of views per video, single
  digit-to-low-double-digit subscriber gains per video, and a Discord in the tens of members by
  end of Month 1. That is normal for this niche and this starting point — the roadmap's job is
  trajectory, not day-90 vanity metrics.

## Phase 0: Foundation (Weeks 1-2)

### Week 1 — Infrastructure, not content
**Production**: No public video yet. This week is entirely setup: Apex Hosting server live at
the 4GB tier (per BUSINESS.md), core plugin stack installed and tested solo, Discord server created
with a minimal but non-empty structure (rules, announcements, #general, a "found-a-house" claim
channel — research confirms empty/silent Discords cause instant churn, so seed 5-10 messages of
lore/rules content before any invite goes out). Create the YouTube channel: banner, About section,
channel trailer slot reserved (fill in Week 3), profile picture. Do one full OBS + CapCut dry run
end-to-end on throwaway footage to find workflow friction *before* it costs a real episode.

**KPIs**: Server uptime confirmed. Discord exists with 10+ seeded messages, not empty. Channel
page complete. Zero public videos — this is expected and correct.

**Skill development**: Learn OBS scene setup (game capture + overlay) and CapCut's basic cut/caption
workflow on disposable footage, not the real launch video.

**Obstacle flag**: The temptation here is to rush to "publish something." Resist — a channel's
first impression (trailer + first video) matters disproportionately for suggested-video
distribution; a rushed, rough first video is harder to recover from than a one-week delay.

### Week 2 — Script and shoot the launch arc
**Production**: Finalize the launch/announcement script (draft already exists at
`scripts/server-launch-announcement.md`) — reveal lore, rules, how to join, tease the House system.
Record gameplay + voiceover, do the first real CapCut edit. Target length: 6-10 minutes — long
enough for meaningful watch time, short enough that a first edit doesn't take 20+ hours. Prepare
1-2 short-form cutdowns from the same footage (a single striking visual moment — a build reveal or
lore hook) for Shorts, since Shorts are the primary top-of-funnel discovery lever available to a
zero-subscriber channel.

**KPIs**: Launch video fully edited and scheduled, not yet published. 1-2 Shorts cut and scheduled.

**Analytics**: None yet — nothing is public.

**Skill development**: First real narration take (expect it to feel awkward — that's normal;
budget for 2-3 retakes of the cold open specifically, since hook quality in the first 30 seconds
disproportionately affects retention).

## Phase 1: Launch Arc (Weeks 3-5)

### Week 3 — Publish, and manufacture the population problem's solution
**Production**: Publish the launch/announcement video + first Short, timed together. Post the
Discord invite in the video description, pinned comment, and community tab (community posts require
~500 subscribers on some channel states but are available earlier via a channel's "Community" tab
eligibility in 2026 for many new channels — verify eligibility on publish day; if not yet unlocked,
use pinned comments and video description as the substitute). Personally recruit the first 5-10
players into the server this week — friends, small-Minecraft-Discord communities, relevant subreddit
threads where self-promotion is allowed — because the next episode's content literally cannot exist
without at least a few Houses staking claims.

**KPIs (be honest, these are small)**: 50-300 views on the launch video is a realistic range for a
zero-subscriber channel with no prior audience; 10-40 subscribers; 5-20 Discord members;
5-15 people actually joining the server. If the video gets fewer than ~50 views in the first
72 hours, that's a signal to revisit title/thumbnail (see Optimization, Week 6) rather than assume
the content itself failed.

**Engagement**: Reply to every single comment and every Discord message personally in week 1 — at
this scale it's fully achievable and it's the highest-leverage engagement action available (small
channels live or die on whether the few people who show up feel seen).

**Analytics — daily check for 48 hours**: watch first-48-hour retention curve and CTR on the launch
video specifically; the first 24-48 hours of real-account watch sessions disproportionately shape
how far YouTube pushes a new video into suggested/browse feeds.

### Week 4 — First House-founding episodes
**Production**: With the first handful of players in, film and cut 1-2 "House founding" episodes —
whichever early recruits are actually building/claiming becomes the footage. Cadence: aim for one
episode this week, not two — solo-creator realism. Cut 2-3 more Shorts from this footage.

**KPIs**: Views likely still in the low hundreds per video; the metric to watch is *not* raw views
but **returning viewers / subscriber view percentage** — early signal of whether the small audience
that found the launch video is coming back, which matters more at this stage than total reach.

**Engagement**: Start a Discord "House roster" pinned post so new joiners see the community is
alive and growing, however slowly.

**Obstacle flag — real one**: with only a handful of players, House-founding footage may feel thin
or repetitive across episodes. Mitigate with narration/editing (frame it as "the first sparks" of
a larger world, cut for tension) rather than waiting for more population before publishing —
waiting risks a content gap that kills channel momentum worse than thin-but-consistent episodes.

### Week 5 — Close the launch arc
**Production**: Final launch-arc episode (3rd-5th House founded, or a mid-arc recap if population
is still thin). Publish 1 long-form + 2-3 Shorts.

**KPIs**: Cumulative totals after the launch arc, realistically: 200-1,000 total views across all
videos, 30-100 subscribers, Discord in the 15-50 range. These are genuinely fine numbers for
week 5 of a cold-start channel in this niche — resist the urge to compare against established
kingdom-SMP channels that launched with a built-in cast.

**Analytics — first weekly review** (see Analytics Cadence section): compare the 3-4 launch-arc
videos against each other on retention and CTR. This is the first real data point set.

## Phase 2: Early Signal (Weeks 6-9)

### Week 6 — First optimization pass
This is the first week with enough data (4-5 published videos) to optimize on real signal rather
than guesswork.

**Optimization actions**:
- **Titles**: Compare CTR across the launch-arc videos. Titles that named a concrete stake
  ("House X founds their keep") vs. vague ones ("Episode 2") — keep whichever pattern performed,
  drop the other.
- **Thumbnails**: If YouTube's "Test and Compare" native A/B tool is unlocked on the channel
  (requires Advanced Features verification), start using it on the next episode: upload 2-3
  thumbnail variants, let it run to statistical significance (roughly 1,000-5,000 impressions per
  variant, up to 2 weeks) — the tool now decides winners by watch-time-per-impression, not raw
  CTR, so a thumbnail that pulls clicks but loses viewers fast will not "win." If Advanced Features
  isn't unlocked yet, do a manual version: swap the thumbnail on the lowest-performing older video
  and watch for a CTR shift over the following week.
- **Descriptions/tags**: Add the server IP, Discord link, and 3-5 relevant search terms
  ("Minecraft faction server," "Minecraft kingdom SMP," "Minecraft roleplay series") to every
  video description — relevance-matching (title/description/tags vs. search query) is still a real
  ranking input even as satisfaction/retention dominate suggested placement.

**Production cadence**: By now, aim to stabilize at **1 long-form video per 1-2 weeks + 2-4 Shorts
per week** cut from the same footage — this matches realistic solo-creator capacity and the
research-backed hybrid cadence (3-5 Shorts + 1-2 long-form/week is the "ideal" full-team version;
scale down the Shorts count, not the long-form investment, when time is scarce).

**KPIs**: Watch for whether Shorts are converting to long-form views (check Shorts' traffic-source
attribution to the channel) — that's the actual test of whether the Shorts strategy is working for
this specific channel, not just accumulating Shorts views in isolation.

### Week 7 — Community tab and Discord engagement ramp
**Engagement focus**: If Community tab is unlocked, start posting 2-3x/week — polls ("which House
should get the next episode?"), behind-the-scenes screenshots, teaser stills. This is low-cost
content that keeps the channel "alive" between long-form uploads without adding to the
production burden. In Discord, formalize a light structure: a weekly "server report" text post
(who claimed what, any skirmishes) that doubles as a content-idea log for the next episode.

**KPIs**: Discord message activity (not just member count) — a Discord with members but no
activity is a warning sign, not a vanity win. Target: at least a few organic (non-Austin) messages
per week by now.

**Analytics**: Weekly review (see cadence section below).

### Week 8 — First real experimentation program
**Experiments to run now that baseline data exists (4-6 videos in)**:
- **Video length**: Try one noticeably shorter episode (4-6 min tight recap) against the recent
  8-12 min norm — compare retention percentage, not just watch time, since a 5-minute video at 70%
  retention can out-rank a 20-minute video at 30% under current ranking logic.
- **Upload time**: Test two different days/times over consecutive uploads (e.g., weekend morning
  vs. weekday evening) and compare early-impression velocity in the first 24-48 hours — for a
  channel this size, "best upload time" is really "when this channel's specific early audience is
  online," which has to be discovered locally, not assumed from generic advice.
- **Thumbnail style**: If not already running, start a deliberate style test — cinematic
  screenshot-style vs. bold-text-over-action-shot — across two episodes to see which pulls higher
  CTR for this specific audience.

**KPIs**: Document each test's result in `content-calendar/` so decisions compound instead of
resetting each month.

### Week 9 — Mid-point check-in
**Analytics — first monthly/strategic review** (see cadence section): pull the last 6-8 weeks of
data. Honest questions to answer: Is subscriber growth trending up video-over-video? Is the server
population actually growing from channel traffic (check Discord join-source if tracked, or simply
ask new joiners how they found it)? Is any single episode format (founding episode vs. conflict
recap vs. Short) meaningfully outperforming others?

**Contingency trigger point**: if by Week 9 total subscribers are still under ~50-75 and Discord
under ~20 active members despite consistent posting, this is the point to activate the
contingency plans below rather than wait for Week 13.

## Phase 3: Compounding (Weeks 10-13)

### Week 10 — Server-driven storylines take the lead
By now the server should have enough real population and history that footage is genuinely
server-driven rather than manufactured — real rivalries, a first real skirmish or betrayal,
alliance drama. **Production shifts from "inventing content" to "curating and dramatizing what
actually happened"** — the intended steady state per BUSINESS.md, where the server is the writers'
room. This is meaningfully less scripting-from-scratch work than Weeks 1-9, which should ease the
solo-creator load somewhat even as editing quality expectations rise.

**KPIs**: Watch whether "real event" episodes outperform "manufactured" earlier episodes on
retention and shares — this is the test of whether the core format thesis is working.

### Week 11 — Skill-development checkpoint
**Explicit skill review** (compare Week 11 output to Week 1-2 output):
- **Editing**: Should show tighter pacing, better use of CapCut's caption/text tools, fewer dead-air
  cuts. If not, consider the DaVinci Resolve step-up mentioned in BUSINESS.md now that basic CapCut
  fluency exists.
- **Narration**: Should sound more natural and less "read," with better pacing around the cold open
  specifically. If narration remains the weak link and is costing disproportionate time/energy,
  this is the point to seriously evaluate AI narration (e.g., ElevenLabs) as a permanent workflow
  swap, not just a fallback.
- **Thumbnails**: Should show a consistent, recognizable style by now (same font/frame treatment)
  rather than a new experiment every episode — consistency itself becomes a subscriber-recognition
  signal in Browse/Suggested feeds.

### Week 12 — Second experimentation round
Re-run the length/thumbnail/upload-time tests from Week 8 with the now-larger data set to confirm
or overturn earlier conclusions — a conclusion drawn from 4-6 videos of data is provisional; a
conclusion holding across 10-12 videos is reliable enough to lock in as a standing production
default.

**KPIs**: Lock in a "default" thumbnail style, target length, and upload day/time as the new
standing practice, documented in `content-calendar/`.

### Week 13 — 90-day retrospective and next-quarter plan
**Analytics — full quarterly review**: total subscribers, total watch hours, progress toward the
1,000/4,000 monetization thresholds (expect to be meaningfully short of both — see milestone math
above — and that is on-plan, not a failure), Discord size and activity, which episode
format/length/style won on retention, which Shorts converted best to long-form.

**Deliverable**: a Q2 (Weeks 14-26) plan that either (a) continues scaling the current format if
growth is tracking toward the 4-7 month subscriber / 6-9 month watch-hour windows, or (b) applies
the contingency pivots below if it isn't.

Do not commit publicly to "weekly uploads" before Week 6-9 proves it's sustainable solo — an
announced cadence that gets broken repeatedly damages trust with the small early audience more
than an honest biweekly cadence that's kept consistently.

## Analytics review cadence (concrete, not vague)

- **First 48 hours after every upload**: CTR and average-view-duration on that video specifically —
  this window disproportionately determines how far YouTube distributes it further.
- **Weekly** (same day each week, e.g. Sunday): Overview tab (views, watch time, subscriber
  delta), Content tab (CTR and traffic-source breakdown across the last 1-2 videos), comment
  themes. Takes 15-20 minutes; do not skip even in weeks with no new upload.
- **Monthly**: Audience tab (returning vs. new viewers, demographics), full traffic-source
  breakdown across the whole library (not just latest video), Shorts-to-long-form conversion,
  Discord growth and activity correlated against video releases.
- **Quarterly** (this document's Week 13, then Weeks 26/39/52): full retrospective — the biggest
  win, the biggest miss, and one structural change to make for the next quarter.

## Obstacles specific to this format, and what to actually do about each

**1. Server cold-start constrains early content.** Solved by design in this roadmap: Weeks 1-2 are
infrastructure-only (no content pressure), Weeks 3-5 accept thinner, more manufactured content
while personally recruiting the first players, and the format doesn't lean on genuine server-driven
drama until Week 10+ when population has had time to build. If population is still near-zero by
Week 6, treat that as the top-priority fix — pause new-episode pressure and spend that week purely
on player recruitment (relevant Discords, subreddits, small cross-promotion) rather than filming
another thin episode.

**2. Solo-creator burnout.** The cadence above is deliberately below what "growth hacker" advice
recommends (daily Shorts, 2-3 long-form/week) because that pace is not sustainable for one person
handling scripting, recording, narration, and editing alone — research puts a single long-form
video at 6-12 hours of post-filming work, and burnout affects the large majority of creators within
their first year. Build in an explicit no-upload buffer week if Weeks 1-13 start slipping — a
missed self-imposed deadline is recoverable; a burned-out creator quitting the channel entirely is
not.

**3. Competing in a crowded faction/kingdom-SMP niche.** Empires SMP proved the format's ceiling,
but it launched with 10+ established creators, each bringing an existing audience — this channel
has none of that. The realistic differentiator is the GoT-adjacent political theming plus "you can
actually join," not production polish (which will lag established channels for months). Lean the
marketing and episode framing hard into "a real throne you can fight for," not into competing on
edit quality with channels that have had years to build workflow and gear.

## Contingency plans if growth is slower than this roadmap projects

Trigger these if, at the Week 9 or Week 13 check-in, subscribers/watch-hours/Discord activity are
meaningfully behind the ranges stated in this document (e.g., under 50 subscribers and under 20
active Discord members by Week 9):

1. **Cadence pivot, not cadence increase.** Counterintuitively, if a video is underperforming,
   the fix is usually not "post more" — it's tightening what's already being posted. Cut episode
   length further (aim for higher retention percentage on a shorter cut) before adding more videos
   to a format that isn't retaining viewers yet.
2. **Shorts-first pivot.** If long-form isn't finding an audience but Shorts are getting any real
   traction, temporarily invert the ratio — lean into 4-6 Shorts/week cut for maximum dramatic
   punch (a single siege moment, a single betrayal line) and treat long-form as monthly "clip
   show" recaps until Shorts build enough of a subscriber base to pull long-form views.
3. **Cross-promotion with same-tier creators, not big names.** Research is consistent that large
   creators rarely collaborate with zero-subscriber channels — the realistic move is finding other
   small (sub-5k) Minecraft or roleplay-SMP creators for split-episode collabs, Discord
   partnerships, or shared "you're welcome to bring your viewers to found a House" invitations.
   This is a two-way trade at this size, which is exactly why it's viable when reaching for bigger
   names isn't.
4. **Reframe the launch-arc content itself.** If the "founding" episodes underperformed, consider a
   clean-slate "Season 2 relaunch" framing once the server has a real population and real drama to
   show — a fresh title/thumbnail treatment and a "the world is different now, here's what you
   missed" recap episode can reset a channel's first impression without abandoning the project.
5. **Format-adjacent pivot, only as a last resort.** If after a genuine 6-month run (beyond this
   90-day window) the serialized-drama format truly isn't finding traction despite the above
   pivots, the fallback is leaning further into shorter, punchier "server event recap" content
   (closer to a highlights show than a documentary series) — still Houses-of-the-Throne branded,
   lower production overhead per video, and easier to sustain solo. This should be a genuinely last
   resort, not a Week 9 panic move — 90 days is not long enough to fairly judge a serialized-drama
   format against itself.
6. **Revisit monetization-adjacent revenue in parallel, not instead.** If ad revenue timelines
   (5-9 months, per the milestone math above) feel too slow, the server side's cosmetic-store
   revenue (per `../got-lore-server/BUSINESS.md`) and a Patreon-style supporter tier can start
   generating small amounts before YouTube Partner Program eligibility — these run in parallel with
   the growth roadmap, not as a replacement for it.
