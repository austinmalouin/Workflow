# YouTube Channel Monetization Blueprint

**Business**: `businesses/minecraft-channel/` (the "Houses of the Throne" narrative series)
**Written**: 2026-07-23 | **Status**: planning document — no channel exists yet, all figures below are
industry benchmarks for comparably-sized/niched channels, not this channel's actuals.

## Read this first: how this relates to the server's own plan

`businesses/got-lore-server/BUSINESS.md` already has a full EULA-compliant monetization plan for
**the Minecraft server itself**: cosmetic rank tiers, cosmetic crate keys, claim convenience,
a supporter tier, and sponsorships once traffic exists. That plan is the source of truth for
server-side monetization — this document does not duplicate it.

This document covers the **YouTube channel's own revenue**, which is a separate P&L with a
different customer relationship (viewer, not player). The two are meant to work as one coordinated
system, not two hands in the same pocket:

- The **channel** monetizes attention (ads, sponsors, affiliate links, channel memberships,
  digital lore products, eventual merch).
- The **server** monetizes participation (cosmetic purchases, supporter tier, claim convenience).
- **Discord is the shared hub** underneath both — see section 9.

Every section below flags explicitly where it touches the server plan so Austin never has to
guess which document governs a given dollar.

## 1. YouTube Partner Program (ad revenue)

**Current (2026) eligibility — verified, not the pre-2025 numbers still floating around older
guides:**

- **Full monetization (ad revenue)**: 1,000 subscribers **and** either 4,000 valid public watch
  hours in the trailing 12 months **or** 10 million valid public Shorts views in the trailing 90
  days. Plus: two-step verification enabled, no active Community Guidelines strikes, channel not
  marked "made for kids."
- **Expanded/early-access tier** (added by YouTube as a lower bar): 500 subscribers, 3 public
  uploads in the last 90 days, and either 3,000 watch hours (12 months) or 3 million Shorts views
  (90 days). This tier does **not** unlock ad revenue — it unlocks Super Thanks, channel
  memberships, and the Shopping tab early. Useful as an interim milestone but not the finish line.
- This matches the BUSINESS.md goal already stated ("1,000 subs / 4,000 watch hours... first
  concrete milestone") — confirmed current, no correction needed there.

**Realistic ad revenue at different subscriber tiers (gaming niche specifically):**

Gaming is one of YouTube's lowest-CPM verticals — skews young (13-24), lower disposable income,
more ad-resistant audience. Current benchmarks:

- **CPM** (what advertisers pay per 1,000 ad impressions): roughly $2-$8, gaming-wide.
- **RPM** (what a creator actually nets per 1,000 *views*, after YouTube's ~45% cut and factoring
  ad-load/non-ad revenue): roughly $1-$3.50 typical, with some estimates up to $5.50 in strong
  months. December/holiday-season RPM commonly spikes 2x a September baseline.
- **Translating to dollars at plausible milestones for this channel:**
  - 1,000 subs / early monetization, ~5,000 views/video: roughly **$5-$20 per video** in ad
    revenue. Not meaningful income — this stage is about qualifying, not earning.
  - Mid-size (25K-50K subs), ~20,000-40,000 views/video, several videos/month: roughly
    **$60-$400/video**, so maybe **$300-$1,500/month** from ads alone if upload cadence holds.
  - Larger (100K+ subs) with strong per-video views (50K-150K): ad revenue starts becoming a real
    secondary income line, but this is 12-24+ months out for a new channel in a competitive niche,
    not a near-term planning number.
- **Takeaway**: ad revenue alone will not fund this channel early. It's the first monetization
  switch to flip (lowest effort, no pitching required) but budget for it being pocket change until
  the channel has real scale. Sponsorships and the server funnel matter more early.

## 2. Sponsorships

**Realistic timeline**: Brands generally start responding to outreach once a channel has
consistent output (not a one-off video) and at least a few thousand real, engaged views per video
— often before hitting 10K subscribers, since brands increasingly buy on *views delivered*, not
subscriber count. For this channel specifically: realistic first inbound/outbound sponsor interest
is **after the launch arc + several ongoing-series episodes are live and showing a repeatable
upload cadence** — treat "3-6 months of consistent uploads with a stable or growing view count" as
the earliest realistic window, not day one.

**Who actually fits a medieval-Minecraft-server narrative channel:**

- **Server hosting companies** — direct fit, and Apex Hosting (already the server's own host per
  `got-lore-server/BUSINESS.md`) runs both a creator sponsorship track ("Team Apex," which includes
  a complimentary server) and a standard affiliate program (see section 3). Worth applying to Team
  Apex specifically once there's a body of episodes to show, since Apex already hosts the actual
  server being featured — this is the single most natural sponsor fit available and doubles as
  in-kind cost savings on hosting.
  - Other Minecraft-adjacent hosts (Shockbyte, BisectHosting, PebbleHost) run comparable
    creator/affiliate programs and are reasonable alternates or additions once Apex relationship
    (if any) is established — don't feel locked to a single host sponsor.
- **Gaming peripherals** (headsets, keyboards, capture cards) — plausible once the channel shows a
  face-optional but "creator" production quality; these deals target creator audiences broadly, not
  Minecraft specifically, so competition for sponsor budget is higher than with hosting companies.
- **Other creator tools** (video editing software, stock asset/music libraries, VPNs that sponsor
  gaming content broadly) — the generic gaming-YouTube sponsor pool; fine as filler once volume
  exists, not a differentiated fit for this channel's medieval/lore angle.
- **Poor fits, skip**: mobile game ad-network sponsors (hyper-casual/gacha ad reads) common on
  generic gaming channels — clash badly with a narrative/lore-driven brand voice and audience
  expects immersion, not a jarring ad-read for an unrelated mobile game.

**Realistic early deal sizes** (nano: 1K-10K subs; micro: 10K-100K subs — this channel will live in
this range for a long time):

- Nano tier: **$100-$500** for an integration/mention, **$250-$1,000** for a dedicated video.
- Micro tier: **$200-$2,000** for an integration, **$1,000-$5,000** for a dedicated video.
- CPM-priced deals: gaming-niche sponsorship integrations typically price at **$15-$40 CPM**;
  dedicated sponsor videos at **$50-$150+ CPM**. Brands pay for *views delivered*, not subscriber
  count, so a smaller channel with genuinely engaged views out-earns a bigger channel with weak
  view-through.
- In-kind deals (free hosting, free gear) are common and worth taking early even at $0 cash — they
  reduce Austin's own costs (e.g., a free/discounted Apex server tier) while a track record builds
  toward paid deals.

## 3. Affiliate marketing

**What fits:**

- **Apex Hosting's own affiliate program** — real and currently open: 15% recurring commission on
  every referred sale, paid monthly via PayPal, free/instant signup, no minimum requirements. This
  is close to a perfect fit structurally: every viewer who watches a server episode and decides to
  spin up their own server (or the server itself needs a plan upgrade Austin is already paying for)
  is a natural affiliate-link moment. Note the distinction from the Team Apex *sponsorship* track in
  section 2 — the affiliate program is self-serve and available immediately, no pitch required, so
  it's the one affiliate relationship to set up on day one regardless of channel size.
- **Gaming peripherals via Amazon Associates** (~20% commission but only a 24-hour cookie window)
  or direct brand affiliate programs — Razer (~10-12% commission, 30-day cookie), Elgato (~6-10%).
  Reasonable as a description-box "gear I use" list once Austin actually has a recording setup
  worth recommending; low expected volume for a channel this size but zero cost to set up.

**What wouldn't fit / skip:**

- Broad "Amazon storefront of random gaming gear" — dilutes a lore/narrative brand and this
  audience isn't shopping for generic gaming accessories mid-video; only include gear genuinely
  used for the channel's own production.
- Minecraft account/skin marketplaces, key-reseller sites, or third-party "cheap Minecraft
  server" comparison affiliate networks unrelated to Apex — actively works against the server
  business (drives viewers toward *other* hosts) and reads as generic monetization spam against a
  narrative-first channel.

## 4. Digital products

Grounded in what *this* channel's audience specifically would want, not a generic "sell a course":

- **House lineage / succession charts** — a visual family-tree-style poster or PDF tracking each
  House's founders, claims, betrayals, and Throne-holders across the series' timeline. This is the
  single most natural digital product for a serialized political-drama format — it's the "who's
  who" reference genre-savvy GoT-adjacent audiences already buy for the real show (family tree
  posters/books are a proven ASOIAF-fandom purchase pattern).
- **Server-world map prints** — an illustrated/stylized map of the actual server world (House
  territories, the Throne holding, key build locations), sold as a digital download or print-on-
  demand poster once the world is developed enough to be worth mapping. Redbubble/print-on-demand
  already has an active market for Minecraft map prints and fantasy-map prints specifically, so
  the production channel (design once, POD platform fulfills) is proven and low-effort to run.
- **Lore compendium / episode companion PDF** — a paid or membership-gated "world bible" (house
  sigils, timeline, glossary of in-universe terms) — cheap to produce (it's a packaging of writing
  Austin is already doing for scripts) and rewards the most-invested fans.
- **Not recommended as a digital product**: standalone build-tutorial guides or redstone guides —
  off-format for a narrative channel (that's the generic-Minecraft-YouTuber playbook this channel
  deliberately isn't running, per BUSINESS.md's format rationale).

Sequencing note: none of this is worth producing before the world/lore has enough material to
chart — realistically after the launch arc + founding episodes, once there's an actual timeline and
map worth selling.

## 5. Memberships (YouTube channel memberships) — tied to the server's supporter tier, not competing with it

This is the one area most likely to create a redundant, confusing "why are there two subscribe
buttons" ask if not designed carefully.

**Eligibility**: YouTube channel memberships require the channel be in the full Partner Program
(1,000 subs / 4,000 watch hours) — the lower 500-sub "expanded" tier unlocks Super Thanks but not
memberships. Up to six price tiers, $0.99-$49.99/month.

**The got-lore-server plan already lists** a "server-name-adjacent merch / Patreon-style supporter
tier tied to the YouTube channel (early video access, Discord supporter role, name in credits)" as
its item #4 — i.e., the server plan *already assumes* the fan-funding relationship lives on the
channel side, not as a separate server-only Patreon. That means the right design is:

- **One membership/supporter product, sold through YouTube channel memberships once eligible**,
  with perks spanning both businesses: early access to episodes (channel-side) + a Discord
  supporter role and small in-game cosmetic-only perk equivalent to what a paying rank would get
  (server-side) + name in credits. This satisfies the server plan's item #4 without Austin having
  to build and maintain a second Patreon page.
- Before YPP eligibility, the *in-game* cosmetic rank tiers (got-lore-server plan item #1) already
  give players a way to pay for cosmetic status — that's sufficient early fan-funding; don't rush a
  redundant Patreon page before the channel even qualifies for memberships.
- Keep the server's cosmetic rank purchase and the channel membership perk **visibly different
  products at different price points** (e.g., server cosmetic rank is a one-time small purchase
  for players who never watch YouTube; channel membership is a monthly viewer perk for people who
  may not even play) — sold from different places, to different intents, so they don't compete on
  the same audience even though some fans will buy both.

## 6. Newsletter

**Realistic use case**: not a general-purpose newsletter — a lore-and-events dispatch. Two
content types map cleanly to what this channel already produces: (a) lore drops (House histories,
map reveals, teasers ahead of the next episode) and (b) server event recaps (siege results,
Throne-holding changes, build-contest winners) for people who play on the server but don't catch
every Discord announcement. This doubles as a retention tool between video uploads and a funnel
back to Discord/the server.

**Platform**: Beehiiv over Substack for this use case — free tier supports up to 2,500
subscribers with no revenue cut and has a built-in ad network that can generate incremental
revenue even before 1,000 subscribers (useful given how slow ad revenue is early per section 1).
Substack's free tier is unlimited subscribers but takes a permanent 10% cut of any paid-newsletter
revenue, which matters less here since a paid newsletter tier isn't recommended in the first place
(see sequencing below — memberships/server supporter tier already cover paid fan-funding; a third
paid product would be redundant).

Sequencing: not worth standing up until there's a launch arc's worth of lore to report on and a
server with actual events to recap — i.e., after launch, not before.

## 7. Online courses / consulting / software — honest "no," and why

Generic monetization templates default to recommending these regardless of niche. For this
specific channel, none of the three are a genuine fit:

- **Online courses** — there is no teachable skill this channel is positioned to sell. It's not a
  tutorial/redstone/how-to-build channel by design (BUSINESS.md explicitly rules that format out);
  a narrative/entertainment channel has no natural "course" to sell because it isn't teaching
  anything. Forcing one (e.g., "how to build a medieval castle") would contradict the channel's own
  format decision and dilute the brand into generic Minecraft-tutorial content.
- **Consulting** — there's no B2B expertise being demonstrated here (this isn't a channel about
  server administration, plugin development, or community-management consulting as its content).
  Nothing about "watch a faction-war drama series" builds the authority needed to sell consulting.
- **Software** — no product-development angle in this business; the channel doesn't build tools,
  plugins, or apps as part of its content. Building software would be an entirely separate venture
  bolted onto a media business, not a natural extension of it.

**Recommendation: do not pursue any of these three.** Revisit only if the format itself changes
(e.g., if Austin later adds a genuinely separate "how I built this server" tutorial spinoff channel
— but that would be a new business, not a monetization add-on to this one).

## 8. Merchandise

Tie to actual lore once there's an audience to sell to — not before. Once Houses, a Throne
mechanic, and House sigils/color schemes exist and have been on screen for a while:

- House-crest apparel/pins (one design per House, doubles as "which House do you support" fandom
  merch — same psychological hook as real sports-team or ASOIAF-house merch)
- Series-branded basics (logo hoodie/tee) — lowest-lore-dependency item, could come slightly
  earlier than House-specific merch since it only needs a channel logo, not a developed world
- Map/lore posters overlap with the digital products in section 4 — the physical print-on-demand
  version of the same map/lineage-chart artwork is the same asset sold through a second channel
  (POD apparel-and-poster platforms like Redbubble handle both digital-adjacent and physical
  formats from one source file)

Sequencing: explicitly **after** an audience exists to buy it — merch before an audience is sunk
cost with no buyers. Realistic gate: launch arc performed well AND there's a stable, engaged
subscriber base in the low tens of thousands, not before.

## 9. Community-building — Discord as the retention engine underneath all of the above

Per `got-lore-server/BUSINESS.md`, Discord is already planned as the server's support/community
hub (announcements, ban appeals, and — relevant here — where store/rank purchases get supported).
For the channel side, Discord should be treated as the connective tissue between every revenue
stream above, not a separate initiative:

- It's where newsletter sign-ups and channel-membership perks (Discord supporter role, per section
  5) actually get delivered and feel tangible between video releases.
- It's where server-event raw material surfaces for the next episode (per got-lore-server's "the
  server *is* the writers' room" framing) — meaning Discord engagement directly feeds the content
  pipeline, not just monetization.
- It's the natural distribution point for lore drops that later become newsletter content and
  eventually digital-product material (lineage charts, maps) — Discord conversations about House
  politics are free market research for what a lore compendium should contain.
- Community retention here is what makes every paid product above (memberships, digital products,
  eventual merch) sellable to a warm audience instead of a cold one — build the Discord community
  in parallel with the channel from day one, even though it's not itself a revenue line.

## Recommended sequence (by stage, not all at once)

| Stage | Audience/milestone | Introduce |
|---|---|---|
| **0 — Pre-launch** | 0 subs, launch arc in production | Discord stood up as the hub (already planned server-side); Apex affiliate link ready to drop in every video description from video #1 (zero cost, zero minimum, immediate) |
| **1 — Launch arc live** | 0 → ~500 subs | Nothing paid yet. Focus entirely on the launch arc + founding episodes. Apply for YouTube's expanded/early-access tier at 500 subs once eligible (unlocks Super Thanks) |
| **2 — Early series** | 500 → 1,000 subs / 4,000 watch hours | Hit full YPP eligibility — flip on ad revenue (expect it to be small, a few dollars to low tens per video). Server-side cosmetic rank tiers (already in got-lore-server plan) are the primary real fan-funding at this stage, not a channel product |
| **3 — Consistent cadence, real views** | Several months of regular uploads, low thousands of views/video | Start Apex "Team Apex" sponsorship outreach (natural first sponsor, doubles as hosting-cost offset); stand up the Beehiiv newsletter once there's a launch arc's worth of lore + first server events to recap |
| **4 — Nano-to-micro creator** | 1K–10K subs, stable/engaged views | First digital product: House lineage/succession chart or lore compendium PDF, once the world timeline is developed enough to chart; broaden sponsorship outreach to gaming-peripheral/creator-tool sponsors as filler alongside Apex |
| **5 — Micro creator, real community** | 10K+ subs, YPP-eligible for memberships | Launch the single combined channel-membership / server-supporter product (section 5) — one purchase, perks spanning both businesses; add gear affiliate links (Amazon/Razer/Elgato) as passive description-box income |
| **6 — Established audience** | Low tens of thousands of subs, developed in-world lore/Houses | Merchandise: House-crest apparel, series-logo basics, physical map/lineage posters via POD |
| **Never (for this channel)** | — | Online courses, consulting, software — see section 7 for why |
